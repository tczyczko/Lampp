Preface

This text is intended to be suitable as an introduction to linear algebra for senior high school and community college students and for those people who desire to learn about the subject but have been away from formal education for awhile. Knowledge of calculus and analytic geometry is not assumed.

Linear algebra is easily defined as the study of linear transformations and vector spaces. This text and its accompanying software package, called Lampp, were designed to help students understand what linear transformations and vectors spaces are, as these concepts are not so easily defined. The emphasis is on applications but great attention is paid to proof as the intention is to allow accommodation without compromise of rigor.

The exercises in this text are divided into two sections, the mandatory (featuring proofs, derivations and problems, which must be accomplished with pen, paper and thought) and the optional (composed of application exercises which can be facilitated by the use of the computer). Answers to selected problems and exercises are given in separate sections near the end of the text.

The text of this book stands alone but is best used in conjunction with Lampp (Linear Algebra Matrix Practice Program)and other software designed to help students solve problems and discover the structure of theorems. The Lampp software can be run as a standalone application and versions are available for most operating systems.

Fields and number systems are not traditionally taught until university. It is hoped that an earlier acquaintance with these concepts will allow teachers to help students view number systems other than the reals in an intuitive manner. The experience and familiarity gained should naturally motivate a desire for further understanding.

The traditional cross product is eschewed in favor of the outer product and the geometric product. This was done to introduce the concepts of geometric algebra. Geometric algebra is a modern approach to mathematical thinking based on the works of Hermann Grassman (1809-1877) and William Kingdon Clifford (1845-1879). David Hestenes is the foremost proponent of this mathematical tool and deserves much praise. It is very unlikely that anyone involved in a future study of linear algebra, differential geometry, projective geometry, vector calculus, tensor analysis, Lie algebra, algebraic geometry, quaternions, complex analysis, 3D modelling or advanced physics will not become acquainted with geometric algebra.

The use of set theory and set notation is minimized in this text as such important concepts are best left to more advanced treatments.

The use of Lampp with this text:

Gauss was said to recall tables of logarithms because it was easier than getting out of his chair to walk across the room to get a book. While this is usually held as an example of his remarkable mental prowess, it also seems to show that even he would have used a book if it had been closer to hand. It is hoped that the use of Lampp will be like bringing the book0.1 closer to hand, freeing the student from mundane, error-prone calculations while allowing them to concentrate on familiarizing themselves with the power of the fundamental concepts of linear algebra.

There are many very good programs already available for solving systems of equations. However, few were designed as teaching aids and those that were emulate fields with the use of floating point numbers, which is an extra level of abstraction which can be avoided.

Lampp was written in such a way that it is impossible to use correctly if one does not understand what fields and row operations are. The user begins by first choosing a predefined field. A matrix of up to 10 by 10 scalars may then be created and the user is allowed to choose which row (or column) operations they wish to perform upon the matrix. Multiplication and addition of field scalars are then facilitated. Predefined fields include Rational numbers, Gaussian (complex with rational coefficients) and Integers modulo a prime. Matrix multiplication and addition are accomplished by showing every intermediate step so that a student (or instructor) may check a problem worked out by hand.

For those who wish to modify Lampp (by adding other fields, for instance) the JavaFX source code is available from the author's Web site. The source code is distributed following the GNU copyleft convention. Lampp has been tested extensively with the exercises in this book but comes with no guarantee.
